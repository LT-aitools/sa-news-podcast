First up: allegations that at least seventeen South African men were lured into the Russia Ukraine conflict and left stranded in the Donbas. Multiple outlets report the men were recruited under the promise of bodyguard or training roles and now say they find themselves on the frontlines. Families are desperate for answers and want clarity about the men who left home expecting civilian work. The recruits say they were misled into signing contracts that compel them to fight, and that they were separated into groups with little information about their whereabouts or the condition of the others. News coverage has named Duduzile Zuma Sambudla, daughter of former president Jacob Zuma, as central to how trust was gained and how the recruitment unfolded; those are the reports circulating in several media accounts. Investigations are under way and there have been reports of police probes, while some commentary asks why the Presidency has been silent as families seek help. In other political news: turmoil inside the Democratic Alliance. South African headlines this week focus on internal disputes between party leader John Steenhuisen and federal finance chair Dion George. The party has launched an internal probe amid accusations that the leader used party resources to purge political opponents. Multiple reports show the DA moving to quell the fallout and to open disciplinary and constitutional investigations into its top leadership. These developments come as the party seeks to manage internal tensions and reassure members and voters ahead of a period of heightened coalition activity in local government. Party leaders say the probe is necessary to restore order, while critics argue it reflects deeper structural and leadership challenges that the party will need to resolve before the next election cycle.

President Cyril Ramaphosa and his team have said the countrys G20 Presidency will cement South Africas role in the global landscape. The Presidency and business commentators note the event showcased South Africas capacity to convene major players and to broker compromise when the international environment is fraught. Delegates are returning home and officials are already talking up the strategic value of hosting the summit on African soil. As with other high profile events, observers will be watching how concrete benefits flow back to the local economy and to regional diplomacy.

Now to an unfolding humanitarian story in KwaZulu Natal. Heavy rains and flash floods have caused death and devastation in the New Hanover area. Reports describe homes and infrastructure badly damaged, at least three people missing or swept away in river floods, and scores of residents displaced. A body has been recovered from a KwaZulu Natal river as search efforts continue for two others. Dozens of households have been forced from informal settlements built on flood plains, and frontline rescuers and local communities are rallying to aid victims. Daily coverage emphasises how quickly conditions deteriorated overnight, the risks facing people living in low lying and informal areas, and the urgent need for relief and safer shelter as weather systems persist. Authorities have urged residents in vulnerable areas to heed warnings and to move to higher ground when possible.

On public services and accountability: concerns are rising over conditions at several hospitals and investigations into alleged corruption. In Mpumalanga, patients have raised the alarm about unhygienic conditions and poor care at Witbank Hospital; the provincial health department has confirmed awareness of ongoing complaints and says inquiries are under way. In separate legal news connected to the Tembisa Hospital looting probe, a Hawks official and a hospital employee have appeared in court accused of attempting to bribe an investigating officer with one hundred thousand rand to avoid prosecution. The court appearance was postponed amid concerns about witness intimidation and to allow further investigation. These stories underline continued public anxiety about hospital governance, cleanliness, and the integrity of investigations into large scale looting of public funds. Health advocates say improved oversight and swift accountability are essential to restore public trust.

Lawmakers say the hearings are part of a broader push to strengthen accountability and to recommend reforms where needed.
