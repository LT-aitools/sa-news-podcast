In education news: momentum is building to end grade repetition for the youngest learners. A document from the Department of Basic Education recommends studying international experience with automatic promotion for pupils in grades one to three. The idea is to prevent early failure from becoming a long term barrier to learning.

Experts warn there is a real risk. Automatic progression without sufficient teacher support and resources could widen existing gaps in literacy and numeracy. The council of education ministers had, in two thousand and nineteen, decided against allowing repeats in the early grades, yet implementation has been delayed. The departments new report signals renewed attention to the issue and suggests policy changes may be on the cards, provided they are accompanied by investment in teacher training and classroom support.

Also in schooling, concerns about the integrity of public examinations persist. The Department of Basic Education has defended the integrity of the two thousand and twenty five matric exams following reports of leaked papers. The department says exam irregularities are not unique to the public system and it rejects claims that the national assessment process was compromised. That dispute will be watched closely by parents and educators as the year begins.

Back home, South African citizens living abroad may face new travel complications after a Constitutional Court ruling in two thousand and twenty five. The judgment appears to have restored South African citizenship to some expatriates who had previously acquired foreign nationality. That outcome has left some travellers in a bind because, technically, those people are again South African citizens and may not be permitted to enter or leave the country on foreign passports. The Department of Home Affairs and the Department of International Relations and Cooperation will need to clarify processes quickly to avoid border snags for families and commuters returning for holidays or work.
