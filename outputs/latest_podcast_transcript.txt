In national politics: concerns about authoritarian narratives and public sentiment. Analysis from Murmur Intelligence highlights a growing stream of political rhetoric that links domestic grievances with a broader, continent wide aspiration for stronger leadership. A recent public meeting involving former president Jacob Zuma and Captain Ibrahim Traore of Burkina Faso has been interpreted by Murmur Intelligence as a visible sign of a cultivated authoritarian narrative. An Afrobarometer survey cited by analysts reports that forty nine percent of respondents now say they support military rule, a dramatic rise in support among some segments of the population. The assessment warns that elements of religious nationalism, anti-foreigner populism and radical movements are amplifying this mood, supported in part by foreign actors and digital media networks that bypass traditional channels. Murmur Intelligence describes this as a phase where those long planted authoritarian ideas may be maturing; that phrasing is the analysts characterization and should be understood as analysis rather than established fact. Democratic institutions and civic groups are being urged to monitor these shifts closely.

Former public enterprises minister Malusi Gigaba has confirmed that he is engaging with an anti corruption body after being summoned in relation to allegations tied to procurement at Transnet. The Investigating Directorate Against Corruption has told reporters that there are currently no formal charges, which provides important context to the summons. Separately, suspended police minister Senzo Mchunu has voluntarily handed over his personal electronic devices to police as part of an ongoing probe. Mchunu also says he offered to step aside from his African National Congress duties but that his offer was declined by the party secretary general. Both developments reflect continuing scrutiny of senior figures and the use of investigative processes to seek clarity on high profile corruption and misconduct allegations.

A major legal settlement is also making headlines: the Please Call Me case. Reporting indicates that Vodacom has reached an out of court settlement with Nkosana Makate over the long running dispute surrounding the invention and commercialisation of the Please Call Me service. Preliminary calculations being discussed in the press suggest the payout could be as high as seven hundred and forty eight million rand. These figures are estimates reported by media and are expected to be confirmed when the company reports interim results. The settlement closes a legal chapter that goes back many years and will have significant financial and reputational consequences for the parties involved.
