First up, South Africas diplomatic response to the United States operation in Venezuela. The reported capture of Venezuelan president Nicolas Maduro by United States forces over the weekend has prompted a firm response from Pretoria. The South African government has called for the United Nations Security Council to urgently convene and has described the military action as a manifest violation of international law, urging the international community to respond. Political parties at home have reacted, with some local groupings reversing previous stances and condemning what they describe as an act of war. Analysts and former diplomats are weighing in, with one former South African ambassador to Venezuela saying the operation appears to be about control of oil and influence, and some commentators warning that South Africa could face geopolitical and economic consequences for its stance. Officials and civil society here will watch closely in the coming days as Pretoria presses for international debate at the United Nations.

Next, South Africans stranded in the Russia Ukraine war zone remain without help. Reporting reveals that seventeen South African men say they were duped into travelling for what they believed was bodyguard training and have been trapped near the frontlines in Russia for five months. They describe a harsh festive season, freezing temperatures, dire conditions and mounting emotional strain. Crucially the group says there has been no meaningful communication or consular support from South African authorities. That lack of contact is the central complaint from those waiting to be repatriated. Families and campaigners are calling on government to make immediate and concrete efforts to secure their return and to communicate clearly about steps being taken. The situation raises questions about consular capacity and the measures the Department of International Relations and Cooperation can deploy in high risk settings. We will continue to follow any official response and updates on repatriation efforts.

In economic and consumer news relief at the pumps is on the way. Retail fuel prices in South Africa are set to fall from Wednesday January seventh with reports saying petrol will reach its cheapest level since two thousand and twenty two. Analysts point out that lower fuel prices are good news for headline inflation because fuel costs feed directly into transport and energy expenses across the economy. Businesses from logistics firms to small shops track fuel movements closely as changes feed through to the price of goods and services. Consumers will be looking for relief at service stations from midweek while economists caution that exchange rate moves and global oil prices could change the picture quickly. For now motorists and households can expect some easing in transport cost pressures if the predicted cuts materialise.

**transition music**

Public health and coastal safety officials are warning people not to eat shellfish washed up on parts of the West Coast. A developing red tide has caused white mussels whelks and other shellfish to wash ashore at St Helena Bay and Elandsbaai. Officials are cautioning the public to avoid consuming these shellfish and teams from the Department of Fisheries are monitoring the situation. Related reports note dead fish in Mossel Bay attributed to sewerage problems and local health warnings have been issued in affected coastal communities. If you live or are holidaying along the West Coast do not collect or eat shellfish from these areas and follow guidance from your nearest local health department. Authorities stress that the toxins associated with red tides can cause serious illness and that cooking does not remove the risk.

There are also concerns at national level about accountability and court activity in Nelson Mandela Bay where high profile criminal trials and municipal corruption cases are expected to shape local politics this year. The start of the school term has renewed debates on education delivery teacher deployments and infrastructure in several provinces and civil society groups are pushing for measurable government commitments on service delivery. Communities will be watching how municipal leadership and law enforcement respond to demands for both improved services and stronger protection of public assets.
