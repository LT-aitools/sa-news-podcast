First up explosive testimony at the Madlanga Commission. Brown Mogotsi, the controversial North West businessman, took the stand and delivered a series of sensational claims that dominated hearings yesterday. Mogotsi told the commission that he had acted as a Crime Intelligence contact agent and said he had posed as an infiltrator to gather information on KwaZulu Natal police commissioner Lieutenant General Nhlanhla Mkhwanazi. He asserted that some of his actions and the text messages now under scrutiny were linked to that alleged intelligence role.

Mogotsi also repeated allegations that link senior policing figures and public officials to wider controversy. His testimony included claims involving the late ambassador Nathi Mthethwa, Zulu King Misuzulu, and allegations about a tenderpreneur known as Vusimusi Cat Matlala. The testimony prompted immediate fallout. The City of Ekurhuleni launched an internal probe into officials implicated by evidence presented at the commission and the metro called an urgent council meeting to discuss the allegations.

Separately in Parliament human rights monitor Dr Mary de Haas appeared before an ad hoc committee probing alleged corruption in the criminal justice system. Several members labelled her evidence as hearsay and gossip after she declined to name sources and used cautious phrasing such as it appears when pressed for proof. The criticism of her testimony and Mogotsi's claims has intensified scrutiny of policing and tender processes and has triggered fresh investigations at municipal and national levels.

In political and legal developments former minister Malusi Gigaba was formally charged in a corruption case linked to Transnet. The Investigating Directorate Against Corruption has added Gigaba as an accused in allegations that include the receipt of cash linked to former Transnet executives and related procurement scandals. The African National Congress has said it expects Gigaba to step aside from party activities in line with party rules after his court appearance. Secretary General Fikile Mbalula confirmed the expectation that Gigaba will step aside though the party has not yet received a formal step aside letter.

The timing of the charges has drawn attention across political circles with Gigaba himself reported as shocked by the timing. The matter will proceed through the courts and it adds to a broader picture of high profile corruption investigations that are shaping political and institutional debates in Pretoria and beyond.

Turning to defence and national security the head of the South African Navy Vice Admiral Monde Lobese issued sharp criticism of budget cuts and what he described as actions that risk South Africas maritime security. Lobese warned that chronic underfunding and fragmented decisions threaten the South African National Defence Force's ability to protect coasts and sovereignty. His comments were framed in stark terms accusing unnamed officials of a kind of sellout that could imperil the countrys security.

Defence Minister Angie Motshekga responded angrily calling the admiral's remarks inappropriate and indicating disciplinary steps could follow. The exchange has exposed a tense debate inside government about defence budgets procurement and oversight and comes as the Hawks investigate alleged fraud theft and corruption in the Navy amounting to nearly five hundred million rand. The public dispute between senior military leadership and the political head of the defence portfolio raises questions about accountability and the impact of spending decisions on national security.

In security and logistics news tied to this weekends G Twenty Leaders Summit Johannesburg authorities are preparing for major restrictions and heightened policing. A hard lockdown will be enforced from Friday in the vicinity of the FNB Stadium and the Nasrec Expo Centre with measures set to remain in place until Monday twenty four November. More than three thousand five hundred newly trained police constables will be deployed across the country to boost security for the summit and the Road Traffic Management Corporation has issued guidance on routes motorists should avoid as the city prepares for international delegations and leaders to arrive.

City officials are also contesting claims about the political handling of public spaces. The City of Johannesburg defended its removal of an unlawful banner along the Moneypoint One motorway and rejected accusations that decisions were influenced by the summit. Meanwhile civil society groups and activists are organising around summit events including calls for a national gender based violence shutdown. The summit is proving to be a test of logistics security coordination and public communication across municipalities and national agencies.

Finally a tragic local story from Gauteng. Two staff members at Inxiweni Primary School in Tembisa were shot dead when a gunman breached the premises. The victims included the school principal and an administrative staff member who were working late when the shooting occurred. Police are investigating the incident and the community has been left in shock. The attack underlines ongoing concerns about school safety and violent crime in towns and townships and has prompted calls for urgent police attention and community support for the grieving families.
